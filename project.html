Is Firm Success Correlated with Female Board Representation?

Introduction

This project will examine female board representation in companies across Europe and whether there is any correlation between reaching equality in terms of males and females holding seats on the board of a company and the success of the firm. Throughout the project, I will observe how gender equality on boards varies between country and sector and I will look to explore if there is a relationship between this and success of firms, using measurements including share price growth and market capitalisation.

Is female board representation correlated with market capitalisation?

One way to measure success is by market capitalisation. As each country has a different number of publicly listed companies, I divided the total market capitalisation by the number of publicly listed countries and ran a regression against the percentage of seats on boards held by females.

As the chart shows, there is a somewhat positive correlation between market capitalisation and female board representation.

Focusing on the U.K.

The U.K. specialises in the financial services industry, which is typically known for its lack of female representation. The Financial Times collected data on gender equality from 50 of the UK's biggest banks, insurers, asset managers and professional services firms.

The chart highlights that, although most companies are employing a roughly equal number of males and females, not one company has reached equality in senior positions.

Is female board representation correlated with share price growth?

The FTSE Women in Leaders Review annually collects data on gender equality in top 100 firms in the U.K. Using the data from the 2021 review and the five year share price growth for the firms in the top 100, I ran a regression to see whether share price growth and female board representation are correlated.

What do investors think?

Despite there being no correlation between share price growth and female board representation, a study by Northwestern University found that gender equality does make a difference to the decisions investors make. They asked 389 investors to bet on whether or not a stock price would increase, telling them that the company either had gender diversity above or below average.
