Is Firm Success Correlated with Female Board Representation?

Introduction

This project will examine female board representation in companies across Europe and whether there is any correlation between reaching equality in terms of males and females holding seats on the board of a company and the success of the firm. Throughout the project, I will observe how gender equality on boards varies between country and sector and I will look to explore if there is a relationship between this and success of firms, using measurements including share price growth and market capitalisation.

Female board representation across Europe

This map of Europe visualises the percentage of seats on boards of the largest publicly listed companies that are held by females. It is immediately clear that Western European countries tend to have higher female board representation than Eastern European countries, with France having the highest percentage of females holding board positions, at 45.3%.

Is female board representation correlated with market capitalisation?

One way to measure success is by market capitalisation. As each country has a different number of publicly listed companies, to ensure fair comparison, I divided the total market capitalisation by the number of publicly listed countries and ran a regression against the percentage of seats on boards held by females. It is evident that, although there is a positive correlation coefficient of 0.18, which is statistically significant at the 95% significance level, this is not a strong correlation.

Focusing on the U.K.

Using a Financial Times article regarding the underrepresentation of women in senior positions in the financial services industry in the UK, I created this bubble chart which compares the percentage of employees that are female with the percentage of senior positions held by females. To gather this data on gender equality from 50 of the UK's bigget banks, insurers, asset managers and professional services firms, I scraped the Financial Times website. The chart highlights that, although most companies are hiring a roughly equal number of males and females, not one company has reached equality in senior positions.

Is female board representation correlated with share price growth?

Annually, the FTSE Women in Leaders Review publishes a report with data on the gender equality statistics in leadership positions of the top 100 firms in the U.K. To obtain the data, I scraped the report, which comes in PDF form, and collected data on the five year share price growth for these firms. I ran a linear regression to estimate whether these two measures are correlated. The results show that there is no detectable correlation between the two variables, although the next chart will suggest that the perceived wisdom is that higher female board representation does have a positive impact on the likelihood of firm success.

What do investors think?

Despite there being no correlation between share price growth and female board representation, a study by Northwestern University found that gender equality does make a difference to the decisions that investors make. They asked 389 investors to bet on whether or not a stock price would increase, telling them that the company either had gender diversity above or below average. When the investors were told that a company had above average gender diversity figures (meaning that female representation in the company was higher than average), 77% of investors predicted a rise in the stock price, whereas when the investors were told that the gender diversity figures were below average, this percentage fell to 25%. This implies that investors not only consider gender diversity figures when making investment decisions, but also that they believe that higher female representation increases the likelihood of the firm succeeding.

Conclusion

Examining firm success is inherently difficult because there is a broad range of measures and definitions that can be used. Looking at success using purely financial measures does not offer the full picture but does highlight that there is a positive impact of having an equal board in terms of gender. More female representation in senior positions means a greater diversity in the decision making of a company as well as new ideas, which can only benefit a firm, and is evidently important to investors. Europe as a whole is taking steps towards achieving gender parity on boards in firms, but there is undoubtedly still a long way to go.
